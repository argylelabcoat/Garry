# The Joyful Kitchen: A Gentle Guide to Cooking from Scratch and Ditching Ultra-Processed Foods

I will tell you something embarrassing: I used to think "cooking from scratch" meant spending four hours in the kitchen every Sunday, owning a dutch oven, and making my own pasta. So I did not bother at all. I bought the bagged salads, the jarred sauces, the pre-shredded cheese, and told myself I was "close enough."

Then I actually read the ingredient list on my favorite salad dressing. And I counted the ingredients I could not pronounce. And I realized I was paying for preservatives, seed oils, and artificial flavors when I could make something better in two minutes with stuff I already had in my pantry.

This is not a manifesto about perfection. This is an invitation to cook a little more and stress a little less.

## What Ultra-Processed Food Is Actually Doing

Let me be precise about what I mean by "ultra-processed." I am not talking about canned beans or frozen broccoli. I am talking about products where the original food has been broken down, chemically modified, and reassembled with stabilizers, emulsifiers, and preservatives.

The research connecting ultra-processed foods (UPFs) to poor health outcomes is substantial and growing. The mechanisms are still being studied, but here is what we know:

**Seed oils at high heat:** Many processed foods use soybean, canola, or sunflower oil, which are rich in omega-6 fatty acids. These oils are often heated to high temperatures during manufacturing, creating oxidation byproducts that contribute to systemic inflammation when consumed regularly.

**Preservatives and the gut microbiome:** Sodium benzoate, potassium sorbate, and other common preservatives do not just keep food shelf-stable—they also disrupt the balance of bacteria in your gut. The gut is increasingly understood as the foundation of immune function, mood regulation, and metabolic health.

**Artificial dyes and neurological effects:** Synthetic food dyes (Red 40, Yellow 5, Blue 1) are petroleum-derived and have been linked to behavioral changes in sensitive individuals, particularly children. They add zero nutritional value—they exist only to make food look more appealing.

**The sugar-salt-fat synergy:** Processed foods are engineered to be hyper-palatable. Manufacturers optimize the ratio of sugar, salt, and fat to override your natural satiety signals, making it nearly impossible to eat "just one serving." This is not a willpower issue—it is food science working against your biology.

## The Therapeutic Act of Cooking

One thing I did not expect when I started cooking more from scratch: how good it would feel.

There is something genuinely grounding about chopping vegetables, stirring a pot, or kneading dough. It is a low-stakes creative act that produces immediate, tangible results. In a world where most of our labor is abstract and screen-mediated, cooking is a rare opportunity to manipulate physical matter and watch it transform.

I do not mean this in a precious, aspirational way. Some nights, cooking from scratch means scrambled eggs and sautéed greens—ten minutes, one pan, done. But even that ten minutes is time spent away from a screen, doing something that directly nourishes me and the people I love.

The mindfulness community talks a lot about "being present." Cooking from scratch is one of the easiest ways to practice presence without trying.

## Gateway Swaps That Actually Stick

If I had tried to go from 100% packaged food to making my own sourdough in a week, I would have burned out before dinner on day two. Sustainable change comes through small, low-friction swaps.

**Ditch the bottled dressing.** This is my highest-leverage recommendation. A basic vinaigrette takes two minutes: three parts olive oil, one part vinegar, a pinch of salt, a grind of pepper. Shake in a jar. Done. You have eliminated seed oils, preservatives, and artificial flavors from something you probably eat daily.

**Make your own bone broth.** Save vegetable scraps and chicken bones in a freezer bag. When the bag is full, cover with water in a pot, add a tablespoon of vinegar, and simmer for 12–24 hours. Strain, cool, and freeze. You now have collagen-rich broth for soups, grains, and sipping—for free.

**Learn one bread recipe.** Not all bread. One. A no-knead loaf requires four ingredients (flour, water, salt, yeast) and about five minutes of active effort. The dough rests overnight while you sleep. You wake up to the smell of real bread and the knowledge that you just made something that most people buy in plastic bags with preservatives they cannot pronounce.

**Cook your beans from dry.** Canned beans are fine—I still use them when I am tired. But dried beans cost a quarter as much, contain no BPA from can linings, and taste noticeably better. Soak overnight, simmer for an hour, and you have beans for the week.

**Grate your own cheese.** Pre-shredded cheese is coated in potato starch and cellulose to prevent clumping. These additives prevent it from melting smoothly. A block of real cheese grated at home melts better and contains exactly two ingredients: milk and rennet.

## Balancing Real Life with Real Food

I know what you are thinking: "This sounds great, but I have a job, kids, and approximately zero spare time."

I hear you. Here is how I make it work without making myself crazy.

**Cook once, eat three times.** I always cook at least double the recipe. Dinner becomes tomorrow's lunch, and lunch becomes the base of another meal. A batch of roasted vegetables on Sunday shows up in grain bowls, omelets, and pasta all week.

**Embrace high-quality shortcuts.** Canned tomatoes, frozen vegetables, jarred tomato paste, good olive oil, and real balsamic vinegar are all processed foods in the technical sense—and I use them constantly. The goal is not zero processing. The goal is fewer ingredients, better ingredients, and no preservatives or additives you cannot identify.

**The 80/20 approach.** I aim for 80% of my calories from whole or minimally processed foods. The other 20% includes takeout, restaurant meals, the occasional bag of chips, and whatever dessert someone brings to a dinner party. This is sustainable. All-or-nothing is not.

**Batch prep ingredients, not meals.** Instead of meal prepping full dishes (which I find boring to eat by day four), I prep components: cooked grains, roasted vegetables, hard-boiled eggs, a sauce or dressing, washed greens. Assembling a fresh meal takes five minutes but feels different every time.

## The Deeper Reason to Cook

There is an argument to be made about health, inflammation, gut microbiome, and longevity. Those arguments are real and well-supported.

But the reason I actually cook from scratch is simpler: I like knowing what is in my food. I like the independence of it. I like that my grandmother's recipes taste the same today as they did fifty years ago because they never depended on a corporate product formulation.

Every time you cook from scratch, you vote with your hands for a different kind of food system. You opt out of the engineered convenience that costs more than its price tag suggests. You reclaim a basic human competence that industrial food convinced us we were too busy for.

And honestly? The food tastes better.
