# Depression, Deficit Spending, and Demand: What Keynesian Economics Teaches Us About Mental Health

I spent an embarrassing portion of my twenties believing that burnout was a character flaw and that the correct response to feeling depleted was to try harder. Push through. Cut expenses. Tighten the belt. It was not until I stumbled into a used copy of Keynes's *The General Theory of Employment, Interest and Money* that I realized I had been running my internal economy like a Victorian banker during a famine.

The parallels are not cute metaphors. They are structurally identical.

## The Boom-Bust Cycle of Human Energy

Keynes observed that capitalist economies do not grow in a smooth line. They oscillate. Periods of expansion and confidence are followed by contractions, sometimes mild, sometimes catastrophic. The error of classical economics—and the error of hustle culture—is the assumption that markets (or people) naturally correct toward equilibrium if left alone.

They do not. They spiral.

When your psychological economy is booming, everything feels efficient. You sleep well, your output is high, your relationships feel nourishing, and you invest in future projects with confidence. The animal spirits are alive. You are operating at full capacity, maybe even above it.

But animal spirits are fickle. A single shock—a rejection, a health scare, a bad performance review, a breakup—can trigger a contraction in confidence. You begin to hoard energy. You cut back on social activity, which was a source of emotional investment. You stop exercising, which was a source of energy dividends. You work harder to compensate, draining reserves further.

This is a liquidity crisis of the mind. And treating it like a moral failing is precisely as useful as blaming a recession on the character of its citizens.

## The Austerity Mindset Trap

The intuitive response to feeling depleted is austerity. Cut discretionary spending. Reduce leisure. Skip the walk. Skip the real meal. Skip calling a friend. Trim the fat.

Keynes would tell you this is exactly wrong.

During a recession, austerity deepens the contraction. Cutting government spending reduces demand, which reduces employment, which reduces demand further, creating a feedback loop that turns a downturn into a depression. The cure becomes worse than the disease.

The same is true internally. When you are already running an emotional deficit, cutting the activities that replenish you—rest, movement, connection, creativity—makes the deficit larger. You cannot austerity your way to surplus. You can only invest your way there.

This is the counterintuitive insight that took me years to internalize. Self-care is not a luxury good. It is counter-cyclical stimulus. When you are down, you need *more* investment in your human capital, not less.

## Deficit Spending on Yourself

Keynes's central prescription for a depressed economy is deficit spending. The government must borrow and spend to inject demand into the system, even if it means running a temporary deficit. The spending creates income, which creates demand, which creates more income, and the economy recovers.

The personal equivalent is doing things that feel like they "cost" energy when you have no energy to spare. Taking the nap. Going for the walk. Calling the friend. Doing the therapy session. These all feel like expenses in the moment. They are actually investments with a Keynesian multiplier effect.

A single restorative walk does not just give you thirty minutes of mental space. It improves your sleep that night, which improves your concentration the next day, which reduces your stress, which makes you more likely to exercise again. The marginal propensity to recover increases with each reinvestment.

Meanwhile, the alternative—pushing harder, cutting leisure, burning the midnight oil—generates a negative multiplier. Each hour of forced productivity reduces the quality of the next hour, which demands more forcing, which reduces quality further. You end up working twice as hard for half the output while feeling progressively worse.

I have done both cycles enough times to recognize them in the first few turns now. The early symptoms of a downturn are not a signal to tighten the belt. They are a signal to spend.

## Liquidity, Slack, and the Buffer Against Shocks

One of Keynes's innovations was the concept of liquidity preference. Households and firms prefer to hold liquid assets (cash) because the future is uncertain. Holding cash is a hedge against shocks.

In psychological terms, liquidity is slack. It is unscheduled time. It is capacity. It is the margin between your commitments and your limits.

Most of us run our internal economies with no liquidity at all. Every hour is accounted for. Every ounce of energy is allocated. We are fully invested in high-risk, high-return emotional assets: demanding jobs, perfectionist standards, packed social calendars.

Then a shock hits. A child gets sick. A project collapses. A relationship fractures. And there is no buffer. You are forced to liquidate at a loss—canceling commitments you care about, disappointing people, burning bridges you built carefully.

The macroeconomic lesson is to maintain a liquidity reserve. The personal lesson is the same. Build slack into your week. Keep some energy unallocated. Learn to say no to opportunities that look good on paper but would leave you fully extended.

This is not laziness. It is a hedge against the uncertainty of being alive.

## The Paradox of Thrift Revisited

Keynes's paradox of thrift states that if everyone tries to save more during a recession, aggregate demand falls, incomes fall, and total savings actually decrease. Individual rationality aggregates into collective irrationality.

The personal version is the paradox of energy hoarding. If you try to conserve energy by withdrawing from life—skipping the social event, canceling the dinner, avoiding the difficult conversation—you conserve nothing. Life shrinks. The sources of renewal disappear. Your energy, which requires stimulation and connection to regenerate, atrophies from lack of use.

The way out is to spend what you think you do not have. To show up. To invest. To trust that the economy of your own life is more resilient than your anxious calculations predict.

This is terrifying advice to give someone who is exhausted. I know because I have been the exhausted person receiving it. But it is also the only advice I have found that actually works.

## Practical Applications of the General Theory

I cannot pretend to have mastered this framework. I still default to austerity when I am scared. But I have built a few rules that help me remember which paradigm I am in.

**Recognize the recession.** The first step is admitting that you are in a downturn and that forcing normal output is not an option. A recession is not a failure of policy. It is a phase of the cycle. Name it without judgment.

**Announce a stimulus package.** When I notice the austerity impulse, I deliberately schedule one thing that feels restorative but optional: a long bath, a walk without a destination, a call with a friend I have been avoiding. I treat it as non-negotiable government spending.

**Stop measuring GDP.** During a downturn, tracking productivity metrics is counterproductive. Output will be low. That is the nature of contractions. The goal is not to maximize production metrics but to stabilize the system and prevent further deterioration.

**Reject the moral framing.** Economic contractions are not punishments. Burnout is not a character defect. The austerity mindset is seductive because it offers the illusion of control through self-blame, but it does not produce recovery. What produces recovery is counter-cyclical investment in the things that make you functional.

## The Animal Spirits of Hope

Keynes wrote about "animal spirits"—the instinctive optimism that drives economic activity. When animal spirits are high, people invest, take risks, and build. When they are low, liquidity is hoarded, opportunity is ignored, and the economy stagnates.

The same is true of the individual. You cannot reason yourself into high animal spirits any more than you can talk an economy out of a recession. But you can create the conditions for recovery by spending on the right things when spending feels hardest.

There is a reason depression is the word for both economic collapse and psychological collapse. They are the same phenomenon playing out at different scales. And the cure for both is not austerity. It is bold, deliberate, counter-cyclical investment in the things that generate life.
