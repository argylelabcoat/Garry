# Zen and the Algorithm: Treating Your Social Media Feed Like a Hostile Garden

You have been told to do a "digital detox" so many times that the phrase has lost all meaning. You have been advised to "curate your feed" in the same anodyne tone people use to recommend a good laundry detergent. You have downloaded the screen-time apps, set the timers, failed, reset them, and felt the quiet shame of being outmatched by a variable-interval reward schedule designed in a Menlo Park conference room by people who read B.F. Skinner the way medieval monks read scripture.

Stop apologizing. The algorithm is not your mirror. It is a garden. And right now, it is a garden full of goddamn thistles.

## The Algorithm Is Not Passive

The standard framing is that the algorithm "learns what you like" and "shows you more of it." This is technically true in the same way that saying a wildfire "learns which way the wind blows" is technically true. It misses the primary action.

The algorithm is not a recommendation engine. It is an optimization engine optimized for a single metric: your continued engagement. Not your happiness. Not your growth. Not your connection to other human beings. Your continued staring at the screen. And the most reliable way to keep a primate staring at a screen is to feed it a rotating diet of fear, outrage, and social comparison, punctuated by just enough intermittent reward to keep the dopamine receptors from fully collapsing.

The algorithm is a garden that grows whatever you water. But it is also a garden that has been deliberately seeded with invasive species. The outrage is not incidental. The comparison traps are not bugs. The way your feed seems to know exactly which insecurity to poke at three in the morning is not a glitch in the matrix. It is the matrix.

And the garden does not care about you. It cares about your attention the way a kudzu vine cares about the structural integrity of the building it is climbing. It will use you. It will consume you. It will leave you feeling emptier and more anxious than before you opened the app, and then it will show you an ad for a meditation subscription.

## Zen Pruning as a Spiritual Practice

The Zen garden is not a product of negligence. It is a product of continuous, deliberate, often violent intervention. Moss is scraped. Stones are placed. Branches are cut back to the trunk. Nothing is left to grow wild. Every element is the result of a decision.

Your feed requires the same treatment.

I want you to reframe the block button. It is not aggression. It is not censorship. It is not even about the person you are blocking. The block button is a pair of shears. When you block an account that makes you feel smaller, dumber, or more afraid, you are not punishing them. You are pruning a weed that was choking the soil. You are acting in the interest of the garden as a whole.

Muting is shears. Unfollowing is shears. The "not interested" button is shears. Every time you use them, you are making a statement about what you will allow to root in the ecosystem of your attention.

This is not a one-time project. A Zen garden is not pruned once. It is pruned constantly. The weeds do not stop growing because you pulled them yesterday. They will be back tomorrow, along with new ones you have never seen. The practice is not to reach a state of a perfectly curated feed. The practice is to stay in the act of curating.

Ten minutes a week. Open your following list. Ask of each account: Does this make my soil richer or poorer? Does this bring light or shade? Does this help me grow toward what I want to become, or does it tangle my roots?

And then cut.

## The Garden Grows What You Water

The corollary to pruning is irrigation. A garden is not defined only by what you remove. It is defined by what you choose to grow.

Most people's feeds are 80% content they do not care about and 20% content they barely tolerate. The algorithm has trained them to believe that this is normal, that the garbage is the price of admission for the occasional gem. It is not.

You can flip the ratio. It takes time. It takes discipline. It takes the willingness to aggressively seek out the accounts that genuinely nourish you—the artist whose work makes you want to make things, the writer whose sentences rearrange something in your chest, the friend whose posts make you feel less alone in the world—and to engage with them deliberately. Like. Comment. Share. Save. The algorithm is a dumb machine. It learns from dumb signals. You can train it, but you have to train it the way you train a stubborn dog: with consistency, patience, and the occasional firm correction.

The rare flowers are out there. The subcultures. The people making things for the love of making them. The communities that have not been fully colonized by the outrage economy. They exist. They are just buried under the kudzu.

Your job is to dig them out and water them until they take over.

## The Violence of the Passivity Narrative

There is a pervasive wellness narrative that your relationship with technology should be one of gentle acceptance. That you should "meet yourself where you are" and "be kind to yourself" about your screen time. That the first step is compassion.

This narrative is, in my opinion, a subtle form of collusion.

Compassion has its place. But there is also a place for righteous anger at a system designed to exploit your attention for profit. There is a place for the fierce, almost violent rejection of a feed that has been engineered to keep you anxious, distracted, and comparing yourself to strangers. There is a place for grabbing the algorithm by the throat and telling it, with absolute clarity, that it does not own you.

The Zen master does not gently accept the weed. The Zen master pulls it out by the root and throws it on the compost heap.

You are allowed to be angry. You are allowed to be aggressive in your curation. You are allowed to treat your digital life as a war for your own attention, because that is exactly what it is.

## The Compost

The weeds you pull do not have to go to waste. The accounts that made you feel small, the feeds that fed you outrage, the platforms that drained your energy—they all taught you something. They showed you what you do not want. They clarified the shape of your own attention by showing you what deforms it.

Compost the bad feed. Let it rot into something useful. The disgust you feel scrolling through an account you should have unfollowed a year ago is data. The boredom is data. The hollow feeling after an hour of thumbing through strangers' carefully curated lives is data. None of it is wasted if you learn from it.

The finest gardens are built on composted waste. The finest attention is built on the same.
